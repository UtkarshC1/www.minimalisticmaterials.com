1. Significance of Classification

The classification of elements helps organize them into groups and periods based on their properties. It simplifies the study and understanding of trends in chemical behavior and predicts the properties of unknown elements.

2. Brief History of the Periodic Table

Dobereiner’s Triads (1829): Elements were grouped in threes, where the middle element's atomic mass was the average of the other two.

Newlands’ Law of Octaves (1864): Every eighth element shared similar properties when arranged by increasing atomic mass.

Mendeleev’s Periodic Table (1869): Elements were arranged by atomic mass, and gaps were left for undiscovered elements.

Modern Periodic Law (1913): The properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers, leading to the modern table arrangement.

3. Modern Periodic Law

The Modern Periodic Law states that the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.

4. Present Form of the Periodic Table

The modern periodic table consists of 7 periods and 18 groups. Elements are organized based on their atomic number.

5. Periodic Trends in Properties of Elements

  • Atomic Radii: Decreases across a period, increases down a group.
  • Ionic Radii: Cations are smaller than parent atoms; anions are larger.
  • Inert Gas Radii: Measured using van der Waals radii, larger than covalent radii.
  • Ionization Enthalpy: Increases across a period, decreases down a group.
  • Electron Gain Enthalpy: Becomes more negative across a period, less negative down a group.
  • Electronegativity: Increases across a period, decreases down a group.
  • Valency: Increases then decreases across a period, remains the same down a group.

6. Nomenclature of Elements (Atomic Number > 100)

For elements with atomic numbers greater than 100, systematic names are based on Latin or Greek roots for digits followed by "-ium."

0 – nil
1 – un
2 – bi
3 – tri
4 – quad
5 – pent
6 – hex
7 – sept
8 – oct
9 – enn

Example: Element 104 is Unnilquadium (Unq), now called Rutherfordium (Rf).
