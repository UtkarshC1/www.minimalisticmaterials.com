1. Elasticity

Definition: Elasticity refers to the property of materials that enables them to return to their original shape and size after the external deforming forces are removed.

Elastic vs. Plastic Behavior:

  • Elastic Behavior: If a material returns to its original shape after the force is removed, it is said to be elastic.
  • Plastic Behavior: If a material does not return to its original shape and remains deformed, it exhibits plastic behavior.

Example: Rubber exhibits high elasticity, while clay shows plasticity after deformation.

Applications: Elastic materials are crucial in designing springs, bridges, and buildings to ensure they can endure stress without permanent deformation.

2. Stress-Strain Relationship

Stress

Definition: Stress is the internal restoring force per unit area exerted by a material in response to an applied force.

Formula: Stress = Force (F) / Area (A)

Unit: Pascal (Pa) or N/m2

Types of Stress:

  • Tensile Stress: Produced when a material is stretched.
    Formula: σt = F/A
  • Compressive Stress: Produced when a material is compressed.
    Formula: Same as tensile stress, but force is inward.
  • Shear Stress: Produced when the force applied is parallel to the surface.
    Formula: σs = F/A

Strain

Definition: Strain is the ratio of the change in dimensions of a material to its original dimensions due to stress.

Formula: Strain = Change in dimension (ΔL) / Original dimension (L)

Unit: Dimensionless (no units).

Types of Strain:

  • Tensile Strain: εt = ΔL/L
  • Volumetric Strain: εv = ΔV/V
  • Shear Strain: γ = Δx/L

3. Hooke's Law

Statement: Within the elastic limit, the stress applied to a material is directly proportional to the strain it produces.

Formula: σ = E × ε

Where:

  • σ = Stress
  • ε = Strain
  • E = Elastic Modulus (specific to the material, e.g., Young’s modulus).

Elastic Limit: The maximum stress a material can withstand without undergoing permanent deformation. Beyond this limit, Hooke's law is no longer applicable, and the material behaves plastically.

Graph of Stress vs. Strain: The graph of stress vs. strain is linear within the elastic limit. The slope of the graph in this region is the elastic modulus of the material.

Beyond the Elastic Limit:

  • Proportional Limit: The point at which the stress-strain curve deviates from linearity.
  • Yield Point: The material begins to deform plastically.
  • Fracture Point: The material breaks.

4. Elastic Moduli

Elastic moduli provide measures of a material's ability to resist deformation under applied forces. The three primary moduli are:

1. Young’s Modulus (Y)

Definition: Young’s modulus is the ratio of tensile stress to tensile strain and is a measure of the stiffness of a material.

Formula: Y = Tensile stress / Tensile strain = F/A / ΔL/L = (F × L) / (A × ΔL)

Unit: Pascal (Pa).

Physical Meaning: A higher Young’s modulus indicates that the material is stiffer, i.e., it deforms less for the same applied force.

Applications: Materials with high Young’s modulus are used in construction (steel), while materials with low Young’s modulus are used in flexible products (rubber).

2. Bulk Modulus (K)

Definition: Bulk modulus is the ratio of volumetric stress (change in pressure) to volumetric strain (change in volume). It quantifies the material's resistance to uniform compression.

Formula: K = Volumetric stress / Volumetric strain = P / (ΔV/V)

Unit: Pascal (Pa).

Physical Meaning: A higher bulk modulus indicates that the material is less compressible (e.g., steel), while a lower bulk modulus means the material is more compressible (e.g., rubber).

Applications: Bulk modulus is important for studying the behavior of fluids and gases under pressure.

3. Shear Modulus (G) or Modulus of Rigidity

Definition: Shear modulus is the ratio of shear stress to shear strain, describing how a material resists shape changes when subjected to shear forces.

Formula: G = Shear stress / Shear strain = F/A / Δx/L

Unit: Pascal (Pa).

Physical Meaning: A higher shear modulus means the material is more rigid and resists deformation due to shear forces.

Applications: Relevant in torsion problems, like the twisting of a shaft.

5. Poisson's Ratio (ν)

Definition: Poisson's ratio is the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain. When a material is stretched or compressed in one direction, it tends to contract or expand in perpendicular directions.

Formula: ν = Lateral strain / Longitudinal strain

Typical values: For most materials, Poisson's ratio is between 0 and 0.5.
ν ≈ 0.25 for steel.
ν = 0.5 for incompressible materials like rubber.

Physical Meaning: A higher Poisson’s ratio means the material experiences significant lateral expansion or contraction when stretched or compressed.

Applications: Important in the design of structures and in understanding material deformation under complex loading conditions.

6. Elastic Energy (Strain Energy)

Definition: Elastic energy is the energy stored in a material when it is deformed elastically. This energy is released when the material returns to its original shape.

Formula for elastic energy: U = 1/2 × Stress × Strain × Volume

For a stretched wire: U = 1/2 × Y × (ΔL/L)² × A × L

Where:

  • Y = Young’s modulus
  • ΔL = Change in length
  • A = Area of cross-section
  • L = Original length.

Physical Meaning: The energy is stored as potential energy due to the deformation, and it can be recovered when the force is removed. It is crucial for problems involving energy conservation and springs.

Example: Elastic energy is stored in a compressed spring, and this energy is released when the spring returns to its original length.
