Fundamental Particles and Atomic Characteristics

Exploring the building blocks of matter

1. Discovery of Subatomic Particles

1.1 Discovery of Electron

J.J. Thomson’s Cathode Ray Experiment:

  • Setup: A discharge tube with low-pressure gas and high voltage applied across electrodes.
  • Observations: Cathode rays traveled from the cathode to the anode, deflected towards the positive plate in an electric field, and could rotate a paddle wheel.
  • Conclusion: Cathode rays are made up of negatively charged particles called electrons.
  • Charge-to-Mass Ratio (e/m): 1.76 × 1011 C/kg.
  • Example: Electron in hydrogen atom: e = 1 .602 × 10−19 C.

1.2 Discovery of Proton

Goldstein’s Anode Ray Experiment:

  • Setup: Modified discharge tube with a perforated cathode.
  • Observations: Anode rays traveling in the opposite direction to cathode rays, deflected in an electric field.
  • Conclusion: Protons are positively charged particles with charge +1.602 × 10−19 C.
  • Example: Proton in a hydrogen atom: p = 1 amu.

1.3 Discovery of Neutron

Chadwick’s Experiment:

  • Setup: Bombarded beryllium (⁴⁹Be) with alpha particles.
  • Observations: Emission of neutral particles with a mass nearly equal to that of a proton.
  • Conclusion: Neutrons are neutral particles.
  • Example: Neutron in helium: n = 1.008 amu.

2. Atomic Number, Isotopes, and Isobars

2.1 Atomic Number (Z)

Number of protons in an atom, representing the identity of the element.

  • Example: Hydrogen (Z = 1) → 1 proton; Carbon (Z = 6) → 6 protons.

2.2 Mass Number (A)

Sum of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

  • Formula: A = Z + Number of Neutrons.
  • Example: Carbon-12: A = 12 (6 protons + 6 neutrons).

2.3 Isotopes

Atoms of the same element with the same Z but different A.

  • Examples: Hydrogen Isotopes: Protium (¹H), Deuterium (²H), Tritium (³H); Carbon Isotopes: 12C, 13C, 14C.

2.4 Isobars

Atoms of different elements with the same mass number but different atomic numbers.

  • Examples: Argon (⁴⁰Ar) and Calcium (⁴⁰Ca).

3. Atomic Models and Their Limitations

3.1 Thomson's Model of Atom

Atom is a positively charged sphere with negatively charged electrons embedded in it.

Limitations: Could not explain the scattering of alpha particles or atomic stability.

3.2 Rutherford's Model of Atom

Gold Foil Experiment: Most alpha particles passed through, a few deflected, and very few rebounded back.

Conclusions: Atom has a small, dense nucleus containing positive charge; electrons revolve around the nucleus.

Limitations: Could not explain electron stability and spectra of atoms.

3.3 Bohr’s Model of Atom

Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits called shells, with quantized energy levels.

Limitations: Failed to explain multi-electron atoms and the dual nature of electrons.

4. Modern Concepts of Atomic Structure

< h3>4.1 Dual Nature of Matter and Light

Light behaves like a wave (electromagnetic spectrum) and also exhibits particle characteristics.

Planck's Equation: E = hν, where h = 6.626 × 10−34 Js.

De Broglie’s Equation: λ = h/(mv), illustrating the wave behavior of particles like electrons.

4.2 Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to determine both the position and momentum of an electron simultaneously.

Formula: Δx ⋅ Δp ≥ h/(4π).

5. Quantum Mechanical Model of the Atom

5.1 Quantum Numbers

  • Principal Quantum Number (n): Shell number.
  • Azimuthal Quantum Number (l): Subshell type (s, p, d, f).
  • Magnetic Quantum Number (ml): Orientation of orbital.
  • Spin Quantum Number (ms): Spin of the electron (+1/2 or -1/2).

6. Rules for Filling Electrons

  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons occupy orbitals in order of increasing energy.
  • Pauli's Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have identical quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s Rule: Electrons occupy degenerate orbitals singly first, with parallel spins.

7. Electronic Configuration and Stability

7.1 Electronic Configuration Examples

  • Oxygen: 1s2 2s2 2p4.
  • Sodium: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s1.

7.2 Stability of Half-Filled and Fully Filled Orbitals

Due to symmetry and exchange energy.

  • Examples: Chromium: 3d5 4s1; Copper: 3d10 4s1.
